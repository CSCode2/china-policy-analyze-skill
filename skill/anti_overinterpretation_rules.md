# Anti-Overinterpretation Rules

## Overview

Overinterpretation is the most common analytical error in policy analysis. It occurs when the analyst reads more into a policy document than the evidence supports. These 12 rules are designed to prevent overinterpretation at every stage of the analysis process.

---

## Rule 1: Do Not Equate "Support" with "Success"

**Principle**: Policy support is a necessary condition for many opportunities, but it is never a sufficient condition for success.

**Explanation**: When a policy document expresses support for an industry, technology, or activity, it means the government has created favorable conditions. It does not mean every participant in that space will succeed. Market competition, execution capability, timing, and many other factors determine actual outcomes.

**Violation example**:
> "The 14th Five-Year Plan supports AI, so investing in AI startups is a guaranteed win."

**Correct formulation**:
> "The 14th Five-Year Plan identifies AI as a strategic priority, which creates favorable policy conditions. However, success depends on technical capability, market positioning, and competitive dynamics. Policy support does not guarantee business success."

---

## Rule 2: Do Not Treat "Pilot" as "National Policy"

**Principle**: A pilot program is an experiment, not a commitment to nationwide implementation.

**Explanation**: Pilot programs (试点) are designed to test whether a policy approach works. They may succeed and be scaled, or they may fail, be modified, or be abandoned. Until a "复制推广" (replication and scaling) document appears, the policy applies only to the pilot zone.

**Violation example**:
> "Beijing is piloting autonomous vehicle regulations, so nationwide AV regulation is imminent."

**Correct formulation**:
> "Beijing is piloting autonomous vehicle regulations. This indicates the direction is being tested, but nationwide implementation depends on pilot outcomes. Monitor for '复制推广' documents before assuming broader application."

---

## Rule 3: Do Not Treat "Planning Target" as "Achieved Result"

**Principle**: A target in a planning document is an aspiration, not an achievement.

**Explanation**: Five-Year Plans and other programmatic documents set targets. History shows that not all targets are met. Some are exceeded, some are met partially, and some are missed. Presenting a planning target as if it were a fait accompli is overinterpretation.

**Violation example**:
> "China will install 1,200 GW of renewable capacity by 2030."

**Correct formulation**:
> "The 14th Five-Year Plan and related documents set a target of approximately 1,200 GW of renewable capacity by 2030. Historical achievement rates vary; the actual outcome will depend on implementation, funding, and technology development."

---

## Rule 4: Do Not Treat Enforcement Action as Complete Prohibition

**Principle**: "严厉打击" (strictly crack down) targets specific violations, not an entire sector.

**Explanation**: Enforcement actions target specific illegal behaviors within a sector. They do not mean the entire sector is being shut down. Even in heavily regulated industries, legal operation continues. Conflating enforcement against violations with prohibition of the entire field is overinterpretation.

**Violation example**:
> "The government is cracking down on crypto, so all blockchain activity is banned."

**Correct formulation**:
> "Multiple regulatory bodies have issued enforcement notices targeting cryptocurrency trading and mining activities. These actions target specific illegal activities, not all blockchain technology applications. Legal blockchain development continues under the '规范发展' framework."

---

## Rule 5: Do Not Infer Direction Change from a Single Document

**Principle**: A single document rarely constitutes a policy direction change. Look for convergence across multiple documents and levels.

**Explanation**: Policy direction changes are usually signaled by multiple converging documents — a Central Committee document, followed by State Council implementation, followed by ministry-specific measures. A single ministry notice or a single provincial document may signal adjustment, but not direction change.

**Violation example**:
> "The NDRC issued a notice adjusting renewable energy subsidies, so the government is abandoning green energy support."

**Correct formulation**:
> "The NDRC issued a notice adjusting renewable energy subsidy mechanisms. This appears to be a policy refinement within the ongoing green energy support direction, not a reversal. The 14th FYP and the most recent Central Economic Work Conference continue to prioritize green transition. Monitor for further documents to confirm whether this is adjustment or direction change."

---

## Rule 6: Do Not Map Foreign Policy Changes Directly to Domestic Impact

**Principle**: The Chinese policy system has significant capacity to buffer, counter, or redirect foreign policy impacts through its own policy responses.

**Explanation**: When a foreign government imposes sanctions, tariffs, or regulatory changes, the direct impact may be partially or fully offset by Chinese policy responses. Do not assume a one-to-one transmission from foreign action to domestic consequence.

**Violation example**:
> "US chip export controls will devastate the Chinese semiconductor industry."

**Correct formulation**:
> "US chip export controls restrict access to certain semiconductor manufacturing equipment. The direct impact depends on the specific scope and enforcement. Chinese policy responses — including increased domestic R&D funding, import substitution programs, and diplomatic engagement — may partially offset the impact. The net effect depends on the interaction of US restrictions and Chinese responses, as well as global supply chain adjustments."

---

## Rule 7: Do Not Equate "Encourage" with "Subsidize"

**Principle**: "鼓励" (encourage) has many forms. Financial subsidy is only one of them and is not guaranteed.

**Explanation**: Policy encouragement can take the form of: regulatory simplification, tax preferences, priority in government procurement, inclusion in demonstration zones, favorable lending conditions, talent attraction policies, or simply reduced administrative barriers. It does not necessarily mean direct financial subsidy.

**Violation example**:
> "The government is encouraging hydrogen energy, so there will be subsidies for hydrogen projects."

**Correct formulation**:
> "The government is encouraging hydrogen energy development. The forms of encouragement may include regulatory simplification, R&D funding, pilot zone designations, or tax preferences. Specific subsidy mechanisms, if any, would be detailed in ministry or provincial implementation documents. Do not assume direct financial subsidy until implementation documents confirm it."

---

## Rule 8: Do Not Treat Short-term Data Fluctuations as Trends

**Principle**: A single data point or a short-term fluctuation does not establish a trend. Trends require sustained direction over multiple periods.

**Explanation**: Monthly or quarterly economic data is subject to seasonal effects, base effects, and random variation. A single month's improvement or deterioration does not confirm a trend reversal.

**Violation example**:
> "PMI rose to 51 in March, confirming economic recovery."

**Correct formulation**:
> "PMI rose to 51 in March from 49.2 in February. This may indicate improvement, but a single month's reading does not confirm a trend. Watch for sustained readings above 50 over multiple months, and consider seasonal factors, before concluding recovery is established."

---

## Rule 9: Do Not Treat Leaders' Speeches as Immediately Effective Policy

**Principle**: A leader's speech signals direction and priority, but it is not a self-executing policy. There is a process from speech to policy.

**Explanation**: After a leader makes an important speech, the relevant ministries and departments must study, draft, and issue implementing documents. This process takes weeks to months. During this gap, the speech indicates direction but does not create enforceable rights or obligations.

**Violation example**:
> "Xi Jinping said China will promote digital economy, so digital economy policies are now in effect."

**Correct formulation**:
> "Xi Jinping's speech signals high-level commitment to digital economy development. However, specific policies, funding, and implementation mechanisms will come through subsequent State Council and ministry documents. The timeline from speech to implementable policy is typically several months."

---

## Rule 10: Do Not Generalize Local Experience to the Whole Country

**Principle**: Local policy experiments reflect local conditions. Success in one location does not guarantee success elsewhere or nationwide applicability.

**Explanation**: Pilot programs and local policy innovations are shaped by local economic structure, fiscal capacity, leadership priorities, and institutional infrastructure. What works in Shenzhen may not work in a resource-dependent prefecture. What works in Shanghai may not be replicable in rural Guizhou.

**Violation example**:
> "Shenzhen's talent policy was successful, so all cities can replicate it."

**Correct formulation**:
> "Shenzhen's talent policy has shown positive results in its specific context — a first-tier city with strong fiscal capacity, a vibrant tech sector, and high human capital concentration. Replicability to other cities depends on local fiscal conditions, industrial structure, and institutional capacity. National scaling would require adaptation to diverse local conditions."

---

## Rule 11: Do Not Treat Market Interpretation as Policy Intent

**Principle**: Markets may interpret policy in ways that serve their interests or reflect their biases. Market interpretation is not evidence of policy intent.

**Explanation**: After a policy document is released, market participants — securities analysts, industry commentators, company IR teams — provide interpretations. These interpretations may be self-serving, incomplete, or simply wrong. They reflect market hopes and fears, not necessarily what the policy drafters intended.

**Violation example**:
> "Analysts say the new regulation effectively bans the industry."

**Correct formulation**:
> "Market analysts have interpreted the new regulation as effectively banning the industry. However, the regulation's text [cite specific language] targets [specific activities], not the entire sector. The divergence between market interpretation and regulatory text may create short-term volatility, but the actual impact depends on enforcement scope and implementation, not market sentiment."

---

## Rule 12: Do Not Treat Absence of Statement as Implied Consent

**Principle**: The absence of an official statement on a topic does not mean the government approves of or tolerates it. There are many reasons for official silence.

**Explanation**: The government may not comment on a topic because: it is still under study, it is not a priority, it falls between jurisdictions, it is being handled through internal channels, or the timing is not right. Silence is not endorsement. Silence is also not prohibition. Silence is simply absence of information.

**Violation example**:
> "The government hasn't commented on this new technology, so it must be allowed."

**Correct formulation**:
> "No official document or statement has addressed this specific technology. The absence of a position could mean: (1) the technology is not yet on the regulatory radar, (2) a position is under development internally, or (3) existing regulations may already apply even without technology-specific guidance. Do not assume permission or prohibition from silence. Monitor for future regulatory signals and apply existing relevant regulations."

## Application Protocol

### When to Apply These Rules

These rules apply to every answer produced by the system. Specifically:

1. **Before finalizing any answer**: Review the answer against all 12 rules. Flag any potential overinterpretation.
2. **When writing the Risks section**: Explicitly note which overinterpretation risks are most relevant to the current analysis.
3. **When providing action suggestions**: Ensure suggestions are conditional and do not rely on overinterpreted evidence.
4. **When comparing across levels**: Ensure local evidence is not overgeneralized (Rule 10).
5. **When interpreting policy language**: Cross-reference with policy_language_lexicon.md and ensure interpretation does not exceed what the wording and context support (Rules 1, 4, 7).

### How to Flag Overinterpretation

When the system detects that its own analysis is at risk of overinterpretation:

1. In the answer, add an explicit note: "注意:此处存在过度解读风险" (Note: overinterpretation risk here)
2. Explain what the overinterpretation would be and why it should be avoided
3. Present the more conservative interpretation
4. If the user explicitly wants the more aggressive interpretation, provide it with full caveat labelling

| "规划目标=已实现" | Rule 3 | Check for planning targets stated without outcome data |
